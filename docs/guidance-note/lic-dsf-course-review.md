## Summary

### Section 1: Overview of the LIC DSF

The IMF and World Bank use two frameworks to assess debt sustainability: the LIC-DSF and the **MAC-DSA (Debt Sustainability Analysis for Market Access Countries)**. What determines which one gets used is the country’s use of **concessional financing** (i.e., financing with more favorable terms than the market rate). LIC-DSF is used for countries that are eligible for concessional financing under poverty reduction and development initiatives through the IMF and World Bank. 

One factor that goes into calculating a country’s “debt carrying capacity” is the World Bank’s **CPIA(Country Policy and Institutional Assessment)**, a rating of policy and institution quality based on 16 indicators, including economic management, structural policy, and social equity indicators. 

Countries with high debt distress risk get concessional funding on grant terms (more favorable) rather than credit terms, but they have to operate under strict limits regarding external borrowing in order to qualify for concessional financing.

The LIC-DSF uses **10 years of historical data** and makes **projections over a 21-year horizon (although only the first 11 years inform the mechanical risk rating)**.

The LIC-DSF contains two DSAs, with some overlap between them. The **external DSA** covers all **external PPG (public and publicly guaranteed) debt** and **external private debt** (non-guaranteed debt private domestic entities owe to foreign creditors), while the **public DSA** covers all **external and domestic PPG debt**. **Domestic private debt** (non-guaranteed debt owed by private domestic entities to domestic creditors) is apparently not covered by either DSA.

Domestic vs. external debt is usually defined in terms of the creditor’s residency in the country, but when that information isn’t available, you can use debt denominated in foreign currency as a proxy for external.

Public debt includes both federal and state/local debt, as well as social security funds, state-owned enterprises (SOEs), and central banks (CBs).

To evaluate debt distress risk, the LIC-DSF uses **PV (present value)** of concessional debt: that is, the **sum of all future payments, both principal and interest, discounted to the present using a standard 5% discount rate**.  This contrasts with the **face value**, the nominal principal amount of the loan.

If **the coupon rate is lower than the discount rate** (i.e., present value is lower than face value), then the loan is considered concessional. The LIC-DSF uses whichever is lower of the PV and face value to calculate the debt burden.

Other types of concessions involve grace periods, maturity schedules, and payment frequency. 

A **debt burden indicator** measures a ratio of debt burden to capacity to pay. The LIC-DSF uses several different debt burden indicators in two categories, **solvency** and **liquidity**: PV/GDP (solvency), PV/exports (solvency), PV/revenue (solvency), service/exports (liquidity), service/revenue (liquidity). In general, **solvency** indicators measure capacity to pay in terms of debt **stocks**, while **liquidity** indicators measure capacity to pay in terms of debt **flows**. 

LIC-DSF computes two mechanical risk ratings: **external risk rating** and **overall risk rating**. When there is supplemental information, judgment may be used to inform the overall risk rating. 

Inputs to the LIC-DSF include **balance of payments stuff** (imports, exports, transfers, remittances, reserves), **government accounts stuff** (expenditures, revenues, assets, and liabilities), **debt stuff** (debt stocks, interest due, amortization due, arrears for external debt, and sound forecasts for existing debt service and future lending terms), and **macrofiscal stuff** (GDP and exchange rates). 

### Section 2: Realism Tools

The 2017 LIC DSF Review found that actual increases in debt levels were much larger than DSA forecasts, and many macro projections were too optimistic. The **realism tools** were introduced to address this weakness.

Basically, the tools contain sanity checks for flagging assessments that are too optimistic or pessimistic. Unless you can justify this by country-specific factors in your writeup accompanying your DSA submission, you have to modify the projection to complete the DSA. 

One strategy used in the realism tools is to **look at what factors contributed to large forecast errors in the past, and what assumptions are used in the current forecast for those factors**. 

Another strategy is to **compare the assumed level of fiscal adjustment with the historical experiences of peer countries**. 

A third strategy **checks the realism of the assumed impact of fiscal adjustment on baseline projected GDP growth**. Typically, for every 1% reduction in a low-income country’s primary fiscal deficit, the country sees a 0.4% reduction in GDP growth. This is called the **fiscal multiplier**, and 0.4 is the average for low-income countries. If they pick a more optimistic multiplier, they have to explain/justify it in their submission.

### Section 3: Stress Tests

Stress tests **look at the sensitivity of baseline debt burden projections to “shocks” like natural disasters**. Specifically, would a shock cause debt burden to exceed some sustainability threshold? 

There are six **standardized stress tests**: 

1. **Real GDP growth shock**
2. **Exports growth shock**
3. **Transfers and foreign investment shock**
4. **Primary balance shock**
5. **Currency depreciation shock**
6. **Combination of all of the above**

The standard approach for each test is to test what happens to the baseline if the shocked variable is **plus or minus one standard deviation from baseline**. The one exception is currency depreciation, where the analysis uses whichever is larger of (1) a 30% nominal depreciation, or (2) the depreciation needed to close the estimated real exchange rate overvaluation gap.

The stress tests do **account for correlations between variables** (e.g., inflation declines if real GDP is lower than expected), but they **don’t account for unplanned policy responses** (e.g., counter-cyclical fiscal stimulus in response to low real GDP). That makes the stress tests a “partial equilibrium analysis” rather than a general equilibrium analysis. 

There is also a **historical scenario** that uses the last 10 years as a realism check on the next 10 years projection. This test doesn’t inform the mechanical risk ratings, though. 

There are also **tailored stress tests** that are tailored to specific countries by accounting for country-specific risks, such as: 

1. **Contingent liability shock** (where the central government has to cover state, local, or state-owned enterprises’ debts). You only include contingent liabilities in this shock analysis if they weren’t already included in the main public debt analysis. 
2. **Natural disaster shock** for countries that experience disasters every 2-3 years with economic loss above 5% of GDP. The shock is designed to increase public debt by 10% of GDP, with a 1.5% real GDP growth loss and a 3.5% decrease in exports from interaction effects. 
3. **Commodity price shock** for countries whose exports were over 50% commodities for the pervious three years. This shock is sized at a a 10% commodity price contraction, with a 0.5% real GDP growth reduction and a 0.75% fiscal revenues to GDP decline. Gaps converge to baseline within 6 years. 
4. **Market financing shock** for countries that do commercial borrowing, consisting of a 3-year, 400-bps increase in borrowing cost, a shortening of maturity to 5 years, and a 15% exchange rate depreciation. 

In some cases, a LIC DSF will also include a **fully customized scenario** for country-specific risks not covered in the standard scenarios, like risk of civil conflict or social unrest.

## Section 4: Stabilization Programs

This section covers how debt carrying capacity thresholds are constructed.

The most commonly used debt burden indicator is debt-to-GDP ratio, but academic studies haven't come to a consensus on what the threshold is beyond which the chance of debt distress increases significantly. Some countries with very high ratios (e.g., Japan) have avoided distress, while others with moderate ratios have experienced distress. The LIC DSF's answer is that the threshold is context-dependent and depends on the country's debt carrying capacity.

Four factors are used to construct the debt capacity threshold:

1. Institutional strength - determined by CPIA rating
2. Global economic environment (world GDP growth)
3. Country income (GDP growth and remittances)
4. Country reserves

Here's the formula for the composite indicator:

CI = 0.385 * CPI + 2.719 * Growth + 4.052 * Reserves - 3.99 * Reserves**2 + 2.022 * Remittances + 13.52 * World Growth

CPIA ends up being the dominant factor (~40% contribution), and reserves the second largest (13-42% contribution). Country growth is the smallest (~4% contribution).

CI scores are updated annually in April and October, when the WEO is released. Note that changing a country's debt carrying capacity classification requires two consecutive signals in the same direction.

There are four debt burden indicators that we test against thresholds:

- Solvency indicators:
    - PV of PPG external devt / GDP
    - PV of PPG external devt / Exports
- Liquidity indicators:
    - PPG external debt service / Exports
    - PPG external debt service / Revenues

A country with strong debt carrying capacity will have twice the threshold of a country with weak debt carrying capacity.

## Section 5: Assessing Mechanical Ratings and the Use of Judgment

It's hard to find the right granularity with a rating system. Ratings with two few categories can conceal more than they reveal, but those with too many categories creates sparse categories whose meaningfulness is hard to measure/verify. The IMF has tended to lean toward fewer categories.

The MAC DSA does not use ratings, but it may do so in future versions.

Most extreme shock is defined as the shock that creates the highest value in any debt indicator over the 11 year projection period. The stress test produces three possible outcomes for any given debt burden indicator:

1. No breach of threshold under baseline or stress test scenarios (low risk).
2. Breach of threshold under stress test but not baseline scenario (moderate risk).
3. Breach of threshold under both baseline and stress test scenarios (high risk).

LIC DSF produces two mechanical risk ratings: external and overall. The external considers the four external debt burden indicators, while the overall considers the four external debt burden indicators plus the PV of total public debt-to-GDP ratio.

For countries with moderate risk, there is also a "moderate risk tool" that measures fiscal space.

- Limited fiscal space: at least one debt burden indicator is so close to its threshold in the baseline scenario that the median shock creates a breach.
- Substantial fiscal space: All indicators are so far from their thresholds in the baseline scenario that a breach results only in the upper quartile of shocks.
- Some space: Anything between limited and substantial.

For market access countries (countries that can borrow on the commercial market), there is a "market financing tool". This tool doesn't inform the mechanical risk ratings, but it may be used to inform the use of judgment. It checks public Gross Financing Need (GFN) against a benchmark of 14% of GDP and the Emerging Market Bond Index (EMBI) spread against a benchmark of 570 bps. A breach of one benchmark signals moderate financing pressure, and a breach of both indicates high financing pressure.

Judgment can be used to determine whether the mechanical risk rating should change the final risk rating. For instance, one-year, short-lived breaches (which are discarded in the mechanical risk rating) might be judged concerning if they are large, or marginal/temporary breaches in the distant future might be discounted because there is a lot of time to plan for them.

High risk on the market financing tool might be concerning enough to affect the final risk rating. Similarly, information from outside the LIC DSF, like private sector distress or exposure that might require government intervention, may be considered. Availability of liquid financial assets to service debt may be considered as a mitigating factor. Long-term risks like climate or environmental collapse may be considered as a risk factor.

There is a debt risk rating category called "in debt distress". This is used when there are outstanding arrears (distress has already occurred). Exceptions are made in cases of voluntary debt restructuring, low arrears, disputed arrears, existing debt relief agreement, or arrears already paid to most creditors and good faith negotiations under way with remaining ones.
