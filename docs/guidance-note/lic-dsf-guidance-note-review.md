# Summary

## Overview

The International Development Association (IDA) uses the LIC DSF to determine grant terms for low-income countries, and Multilateral Development Banks (MDBs) use it to inform their lending decisions. IDA's Non-Concessional Borrowing Policy and IMF's Debt Limit Policy also use it to set limits on non-concessional borrowing.

It looks like the CPIA classifications inform the debt thresholds, and the stress tests inform the rules for mechanical risk signals. These both then inform the external debt risk rating. There is also a complementary domestic debt vulnerability assessment that informs both the external and overall risk ratings.

If external debt risk is moderate, we also trigger the "moderate risk module", which assesses whether the space to absorb a shock is substantial or limited.

This guidance note is intended to be a step-by-step guide for practitioners to use the LIC DSF. A separate user guide and templates can be found at http://www.imf.org/external/pubs/ft/dsa/lic.aspx and
http://www.worldbank.org/en/topic/debt/brief/dsf.

## DSF Procedures

The LIC DSF should be used for all countries eligible for Poverty Reduction and Growth Trust (PRGT) and/or IDA grant assistance.

Countries may "graduate" to MAC DSA, but only after making sure financing projections are realistic and concessional debt share indicators are in the MAC DSA range (see Figure 2 on p. 9 for ranges). If serious short-term vulnerabilities are identified, graduation can be delayed. In general, MAC users have more domestic public debt than external debt, and very small GDP/total debt shares of grant financing and concessional debt.

DSAs are produced jointly by the IMF and World Bank once per year and must be approved by Bank, Fund, and IDA executive boards. New DSAs must accompany requests for IMF/World Bank financing, proposed changes to performance criteria, and after economic shocks. The DSA must also be accompanied by a writeup.

## Inputs to the DSF

### Public Sector Debt

The LIC DSF's ratings are primarily based on external public and publicly guaranteed (PPG) debt. Public-sector debt includes both federal and state/local debt, as well as social security funds, non-financial state-owned enterprises (SOEs), and central banks (CBs). Also any long-term obligations like unfunded entitlements (e.g., Social Security) or accounts payable not included in general government debt. Coverage of public debt must be near-complete, including private sector debt guaranteed by the public sector. Financial SOEs and central bank debt/swaps issued for reserves management purposes are excluded but may optionally be considered as contingent liabilities. Any other omissions must be explicitly flagged and justified and will automatically trigger a contingent liabilities stress test.

Note that there must be accounting consistency between the credit and debit sides of the ledger, so SOEs included in debt must also be included in income accounts.

If there are gaps in data about amounts and terms of loans, this must be included in the writeup and remedial measures to improve data collection should be proposed.

#### Definitions

The concept we are trying to capture with debt inputs is "face value (not market value) of gross public debt". Current debt should include only outstanding and disbursed loans, while projections should include a best estimate of contracted and expected future loans given medium-term government investment plans.

Public sector debt should be consolidated to avoid double-counting (e.g., exclude federal loans to states because these are internal to the "general government"). Disputed liabilities may be excluded but should be treated as contingent liabilities for purposes of stress tests. Claims slated for debt relief may also be excluded.

In theory, we define external debt based on the residency of the creditor. In practice, we can use debt denominated in foreign currency as a proxy since LICs often don't have good data on the residency of their creditors.

### Macroeconomic Projections

Users must provide key macroeconomic projections informed by a macroeconomic framework-- often referred to as a "baseline scenario". User must provide 10 years of historical data and 20 years of projections representing the most likely scenario given current information.

Medium-term projections (next 5 years) should be based on current policy and consistent with surveillance findings and agreed-upon adjustment programs. Any real effective exchange rate gap should be assumed to close. IMF typically takes the lead here.

Longer-term projections (beyond 5 years) should be informed by typical patterns for developing countries. World Bank typically takes the lead here.

#### Macroeconomic Variables

Broadly, the macroeconomic inputs fall into four categories:

- Balance of payments
- Public sector stocks and flows
- Debt stocks and flows
- Other (GDP, deflator, exchange rates, investment, etc.)

### Financing Assumptions

LIC DSF requires info on:

- Existing debt
- Planned new borrowing

Planned new borrowing must be consistent with macroeconomic forecasts.

External financing is broken out into multilateral, bilateral, and commercial line items. Domestic financing is broken down into central bank advances vs. private sources. Additionally, you should break down by debt instrument source and specify average interest rate, grace period, and maturity for each instrument.

The DSA baseline projection should only include firmly committed grants and debt relief. It can include highly likely grants as long as their inclusion doesn't change the final debt risk rating (because the risk rating is an input for grant decisions, so this gets circular). For companies seeking HIPC debt relief but not yet approved, it should be reflected in a customized scenario rather than the baseline forecast.

#### Financing Variables

- New disbursements of PPG external debt
- New disbursements of domestic debt

## Realism Tools

The LIC DSF has four realism tools. Their purpose is not to be prescriptive, but rather to surface problems with the baseline forecast and its assumptions. Where they flag potential realism problems, the macro projections should either be revised or justified in terms of country-specific factors in the writeup.

- **Drivers of debt dynamics** - Checks last year and 5 years ago DSAs against outcomes and shows charts of drivers of debt dynamics and composition of past forecast errors. If forecasts differ sharply from historical data or there were large forecasting errors in the past, the check flags this and enables users to identify what drove it so this/these factor(s) can be given special scrutiny.
- **Realism of planned fisal adjustment** - Compared projected fiscal adjustment against cross-country experience with fiscal adjustment. If this check throws a flag, the user will need to revise baseline or to justify (for example, defend the political feasibility of planned adjustment).
- **Fiscal adjustment-growth relationship** - Compares baseline forecast against what that forecast would look like if we used last growth observation carried forward, as well as with various fiscal multipliers (that is, the multiple by which fiscal adjustment affects growth, typically 0.4 for low-income countries). If this check throws a flag, the user will need to revise baseline or to justify (for example, in terms of "positive shocks" like structural reforms).
- **Public investment-growth relationship** - Checks historical contribution of public investment to growth against previous and current projections to ensure investment efficiency assumptions are realistic.

Additionally, the user is encouraged to compare their baseline forecast against forecasts from other institutions.

## Debt Carrying Capacity

The DSF uses a "composite indicator" to assess debt carrying capacity. It is a weighted average of the following indicators, informed by World Bank CPIA scores and the IMF's WEO:

- CPIA score
- Real GDP growth
- Remittances
- Reserves
- World growth

Formula: CI = β1 CPIA + β2 g + β3 Remittances/GDP + β4 Reserves/Imports + β5 (Reserves/Imports)**2 + β6 gw

Coefficient estimates for this model: β1 = 0.385; β2 = 2.719; β3 = 2.022; β4 = 4.052; β5 = −3.990; β6 = 13.520

The composite indicator calculation is based on a 10-year average of each variable, informed by 5 years of historical and 5 years of projection data.

The DSF shows the CI score and the contribution of each variable to the composite indicator. Depending on the CI score, the DSF will assign a debt carrying capacity rating of weak, medium, or strong:

- CI < 2.69 - Weak
- 2.69 ≤ CI ≤ 3.05 - Medium
- CI > 3.05 - Strong

Once a country has been classified, there must be two consecutive designations in a new category before it can be reclassified. E.g., a medium debt carrying capacity country as of October must rate as strong in both the April and October DSA updates to be reclassified as strong.

### Thresholds for PPG External Debt

The DSF uses a statistically determined threshold, based on the debt carrying capacity rating, to analyze risk of debt distress. If PPG external debt crosses the threshold, risk of debt distress is elevated.

## Stress Tests

There are three types of stress tests:

- Standardized stress tests
- Tailored stress tests
- Fully customized stress tests (optional)

The most extreme stress test informs the calculation of the mechanical risk signal, such that where the test breaches the debt threshold, the signal shifts from "low" to "moderate" risk.

### Standardized Stress Tests

There are six standardized stress tests, all of which contribute to both the external and overall debt risk ratings.

The stress tests generally involve "shocking" a variable by +/- 1 SD and see if thresholds are violated. For each variable, we shock either the baseline projection or the historical average, whichever is lower (and thus avoid penalizing conservative forecasts). Shock size can be (but usually is not) customized based on country-specific factors.

Interactions between correlated variables are modeled based on assumptions about cross-elasticities; e.g., exchange rate shock causes higher inflation and net exports. Elasticities can be customized based on country-specific factors.

- **Real GDP growth shock** - Shock real GDP growth by +/- 1 SD in the second and third years of the projection period, with elasticity to inflation of 0.6 and elasticity to non-interest expenditures of 1.0. Increase domestic borrowing cost by 25 bps per 1% of GDP (which worsens primary balance for LICs with domestic market financing). Increase external market borrowing cost by 400 bps or 100 bps per 1% of GDP, whichever is lower.
- **Primary balance shock** - Shock primary balance-to-GDP ratio by +/- 1 SD in the second and third years of the projection period.
- **Exports growth shock** - Shock exports growth by - 1 SD in the second and third years of the projection period. Reduce GDP growth with an elasticity to exports of 0.8.
- **Other flows shock** - Shock current transfers-to-GDP ratio and foreign direct investment-to-GDP ratios by -1 SD.
- **Depreciation shock** - Shock nominal domestic currency depreciation to 30% or size of real exchange rate gap, whichever is larger, in year 2 of projection period. Adjust real net exports with elasticity to real depreciation of 0.15 in the next year, and pass through to inflation with elasticity of 0.3 in year of shock.
- **Combination of all of the above** - Apply all individual shocks at half the magnitude.

### Historical Scenario

The historical scenario gives the debt path that would be produced if we replaced key macro variables in the forecast with their 10-year historical averages. This doesn't affect the mechanical risk rating, but it does provide a realism check on the forecast. Plausible reasons for deviation might include structural changes like the end of civil war.

### Contingent Libaility Stress Test

Applies a one-off increase in debt-to-GDP ratio in year 2 of projection period, with a minimum of 5% and a tailored value for any public sector liabilities not included in the DSA's definition of public debt. Size of this tailored shock should be based on country-specific factors, but defaults are 5% of GDP for financial sector, 0% of GDP for general government, 2% of GDP for SOEs, and 35% of PPP capital stock for public-private partnerships (only if larger than 3% of GDP). Writeup must explain design of the tailored shocks.

### Tailored Stress Tests

The LIC DSF template automatically detects, based on user inputs, whether to include tailored shocks.

- **Natural disaster shock** - Applies to small states vulnerable to natural disasters and LICs with a disaster-related economic loss exceeding 5% of GDP with a frequency exceeding 2 disasters every 3 years from 1950 to 2015 (based on EM-DAT database). If natural disaster impacts are already included in the baseline scenario, users should subtract them from the year 2 shock. Debt-to-GDP ratio is shocked by 10% of GDP in year 2, with a 1.5% real GDP growth loss and a 3.5% decrease in exports in the year of the shock.
- **Commodity price shock** - Applies to LICs where commodities were at least 50% of total exports over the last three years. Shocks commodity export prices by -1 SD, and the price then converges to baseline over 6 years. (If the country is also an importer of a shocked commodity, net export may be used.) Real GDP growth is reduced by 0.5% and fiscal revenues-to-GDP ratio by 0.75% for each 10% commodity price contraction in the three years starting from the year of the shock. This converges to baseline in 6 years. GDP deflator is reduced by the size of the commodity price shock, converging to baseline in 6 years.
- **Market financing shock** - Applies to LICs with outstanding Eurobonds or that meet the criteria for Poverty Reduction and Growth Trust (PRGT) graduation but have not yet graduated. Assesses rollover risks from a commercial bond repricing event of 400 bps, sustained for 3 years from year 2. Maturity is shortened to 5 years or 2/3 of the remaining maturity, whichever is shorter. Exchange rate is depreciated by 15% in year 2 of projection period.

Users are expected to customize the scenarios for these tailored stress tests, e.g., based on historical experience of the country. Parameter choices should be explained in the writeup.

### Fully Customized Scenarios

The template allows for the creation of scenarios that fully customize the PPG external and public debt paths for idiosyncratic country-specific risks (like epidemic, civil war, or policy slippage). They affect the debt rating the same way standardized stress tests do.

A customized scenario can also be used for illustrative purposes to illustrate the positive implications of possible grants.

## Risk Signals

The external debt distress signal is derived by comparing the four projected PPG external debt burden indicators with their thresholds in the most extreme stress test. The signal takes a value of low, medium, or high risk of external debt distress:

- Low: PPG external debt burden indicators do not breach their respective thresholds in the most extreme stress test.
- Medium: No PPG external debt indicators breach thresholds in the baseline scenario, but at least one breaches theshold in the most extreme stress test.
- High: At least on PPG external debt indicator breaches threshold in the baseline scenario.

The overall risk signal follows the same methodology but adds the PV of total public debt-to-GDP ratio as a fifth debt burden indicator on top of the four PPG external debt burden indicators.

## Signal from the Market Financing Pressures Tool

For LICs with market access, there's an additional signal that compared public financing needs over the next three years and the current EMBI spread against these benchmarks:

| Public GFN | EMBI spread |
| 14% of GDP | 570 bps |

A breach of one benchmark signals moderate financing pressure, and a breach of both indicates high financing pressure.

## Use of Judgment

The final risk rating must take into account not only the mechanical risk rating, but also human judgment. For instance, short-lived (1-year) breaches are disregarded by the mechanical risk rating, but human judgment may bring them back if they occur in the very near future. Also, marginal breaches of thresholds may be discounted if there are sufficient mitigating factors.

If a high-risk signal comes from public debt distress (as opposed to external debt distress), the analyst may discount this if: 1. external debt is defined by FX-denomination, not by residency, and 2. some due diligence finds that non-residents are not an important share of public debt.

The market financing pressures tool does not affect mechanical risk ratings, so the analyst must make a judgment about whether a high risk signal from this tool rises to a level that raises the risk of external and overall public debt distress.

The user should always consider whether contingent liabilities (e.g., non-guaranteed private external debt) increase exposure to levels that should raise risk ratings. Pay particular attention if private commercial debt is rising or projected to rise rapidly and to whether the private sector has sufficient liquidity to mitigate risks. Otherwise, government intervention might be required, which would directly addect public debt issuance.

The availability of government assets already earmarked for debt service or that could be liquidated to service debt should be considered as offsets to debt risk. The assets should be liquid and currency denomination should be considered. This should be reflected in the writeup.

Only the next 10 years of projections are used to inform the mechanical risk rating, but the user may choose to adjust the risk rating based on breaches in years 11-20 if such breaches are expected to be large, persistent, and difficult to mitigate (e.g., due to demographics or climate change).

Other factors that may require exercise of judgment include:

- Fragility, conflict and violence (FCV) risks
- Reserve pooling (which gives greater balance of payments protection unless the union itself is at risk or the country has lost pool access)
- Availability of insurance
- Baseline forecast confidence

## Final Risk Ratings

The external debt risk rating is considered the primary DSF output, while the overall rating is supplemental.

In addition to "low", "moderate", and "high", a country can also be rated "in debt distress" if there are outstanding arrears (distress has already occurred). There are some exceptions made in cases where arrears are small or disputed or good faith debt restructuring is mostly completed already. Arrears to domestic suppliers can also trigger an "in debt distress" rating if it's judged to be due to government insolvency or liquidity problems.

LIC DSF writeup should indicate limitations of and/or risks to the rating judgment, such as data coverage or forecast uncertainty.

## Adding Granularity to Risk Ratings

The user must characterize the extent of vulnerability in a moderate-risk country. This is a function of "fiscal space" a country has to absorb shocks without violating thresholds in the baseline forecast and being downgraded to high risk. The user should characterize fiscal space as "limited", "substantial", or "some".

The LIC DSF provides tools to assess fiscal space, although it does not require their use, and they do not support full fiscal space assessment.

## Stages of Preparation for a DSA

1. IMF country teams and World Bank country economists jointly prepare a draft DSA and meet to discuss assumptions.
2. The draft is circulated to SPR, MTI Global Macro, and the Debt Analytics Unit for formal review.
3. Disagreements are discussed at a Policy Consultation Meeting (PCM) and disputes resolved.
4. IMF and World Bank management clear the draft.
5. IMF and World Bank country teams refine the draft in collaboration with country authorities (the "mission" phase).
6. Departmental review and management clearance of the staff report and revised draft.
7. IMF Executive Board and IDA Executive Board approval and publication.

## DSA writeup

The writeup should include:

- Key risk ratings
- Use of judgment
- Risk rating granularity

It also should discuss:

- Public debt coverage
- Recent development and composition of debt burden
- Background on macro forecasts, including assumptions used and realism tool outputs
- Stress tests used and explanation of tailoring or customization
- Country authorities' views

## Treatment of SOEs

- Can be excluded if it can borrow externally without a public guarantee or otherwise poses little fiscal risk.

## Long-Term (>5 years) Macroeconomic Projections and Financing Assumptions

- Empirical evidence shows that fiscal adjustment policy and inflation dynamics improvements are rarely sustained, so you should be careful not to assume this over the long term unless there have been structural changes to institutions.
- Long-term growth projections need to be realistic for the country's stage of development and must reflect natural disasters, domestic instability, and resource production lags and constraints.
- Financing assumptions should be anchored to the government's medium-term debt management strategy (MTDS).
- Countries can also be grouped broadly by market maturity (low, moderate, and mature) and debt mixes shift over time as they advance through these stages.

## Borderline Cases

In borderline cases, the template will detect that you need to use an alternative "probability of debt distress" calculation. The user can also opt in to use the probability approach as a complementary tool to inform judgment in borderline cases.
